- Call out specific, recurring anti-patterns I’m seeing in large orgs trying to move from "LLM demos" to "AI systems"—committee-ware, platform worship, and fake experiments.

## Draft Post
Slide 1 – Title
What Steve Yegge’s Google AI story gets right (and wrong) about enterprise AI adoption

Subtitle: Lessons from building real AI systems, not just LLM demos

---
Slide 2 – Quick recap (for context)
Steve Yegge tells a wild story:
- A senior Google tech director vents about AI
- Endless reviews, policy blockers, security gates
- Teams can’t just ship AI-powered stuff, even with great infra

The takeaway people latched onto:
“Google is too bureaucratic to adopt AI.”

That’s true.
But it’s also too shallow.

---
Slide 3 – What Yegge gets exactly right
Three things he nails about big-company AI:

1) AI is treated as a **“dangerous plugin”**
   – Every workflow change triggers legal, security, policy
   – Default stance: assume risk, demand exception

2) Decision power is **orthogonal to AI competence**
   – Policy committees with no builders
   – Model and infra teams have influence, not authority

3) Velocity is killed by **meta-work**
   – Reviews, decks, steering groups
   – More approval flows than actual AI flows

If you’ve tried shipping AI in an enterprise, this is familiar.

---
Slide 4 – Where the story is misleading
The viral reading is:
“Google is slow because it’s ‘too safe’. Just be less risk-averse.”

That’s not the interesting part.

The non-obvious reality I see in enterprises:
- The real constraint isn’t **caution**
- It’s **organizational refusal to change how work happens**

You can loosen policy all you want.
If:
- Roles don’t change,
- Metrics don’t change,
- Systems of record don’t change,

…your AI program will still stall.

---
Slide 5 – Mistake #1: Treating AI as a feature, not an operating change
Most big companies do this:

"Let’s add an LLM feature to our CRM / helpdesk / BI tool."

What this misses:
- AI systems don’t add a pixel; they **change who does the work**
- That means changing:
  – Ownership (who’s responsible when AI acts?)
  – Control (what can be automated, by whom?)
  – Escalation (how do humans overrule?)

If you don’t touch process, permissions, and accountability:
You don’t have AI systems.
You have an expensive autocomplete.

---
Slide 6 – Mistake #2: Platform worship
Yegge’s story emphasizes infra and tools.
Most enterprises copy that mistake:

- “We need an AI platform first”
- 18-month roadmap
- Vendor bakeoffs, RFPs, architecture boards

Pattern I see in the wild:
- Infra teams think in terms of **capabilities**
- The business needs **decisions and actions** changed

Consequence:
- Beautiful platform
- Trivial use cases
- Zero impact on how the company actually operates

If your AI platform doesn’t break or rewrite at least one critical workflow, it’s a cost center, not a transformation.

---
Slide 7 – Mistake #3: Fake experiments
The loudest internal message after Yegge’s story was:
"We need more AI experimentation."

Most orgs translate that into:
- Hackathons
- Demo days
- Sandboxes with no production data

That’s not experimentation.
That’s theater.

Real experiments:
- Touch live workflows (even if only for 1% of traffic)
- Measure latency, error rates, escalation load
- Force you to negotiate with risk, compliance, and ops

If your "pilot" couldn’t possibly break something important, it can’t teach you how to adopt AI.

---
Slide 8 – What actually works: a pattern I see in successful teams
The teams that escape Google-style paralysis do almost the opposite:

1) Start from a **specific decision or workflow**
   – "Triage support tickets"
   – "Draft responses for KYC edge-cases"
   – "Summarize weekly risk incidents for leadership"

2) Design the **operating model**, not the model call
   – What’s the AI agent allowed to do?
   – When must a human review?
   – How do we log, audit, and override?

3) Ship a constrained agent into production quickly
   – Narrow scope, dirty plumbing
   – Real users, real stakes, reversible blast radius

4) Then worry about platform
   – Standardize patterns that actually worked
   – Codify guardrails after they’ve been battle-tested

---
Slide 9 – The non-obvious pattern: AI adoption is an org design problem
If you strip away the Google drama, a simple pattern emerges:

- Small companies treat AI as **leverage**
- Big companies treat AI as **surface area**

Same tech, opposite behavior.

The orgs that win don’t have better models.
They have:
- Fewer approval hops between builder and production
- Leaders willing to redraw swimlanes and KPIs
- A bias toward changing workflows, not just UIs

That’s the part Yegge’s story gestures at but doesn’t spell out.

---
Slide 10 – A quick diagnostic for your org
Three brutally honest questions:

1) Name one core workflow where:
   – AI now does work a human used to do
   – Metrics, ownership, and escalation paths changed

2) How many approvals does it take to ship a new AI behavior to production?
   – Count people, not Jira tickets

3) Is your “AI strategy” deck mostly about:
   – Tools, models, vendors
   – Or workflows, outcomes, and changed responsibilities?

If you can’t answer (1), or (2) > 5 people, you’re on track to recreate Google’s AI pain — just with less talent and fewer GPUs.

That’s fixable. But not with more models.
Only with more willingness to rewire how your org actually works.
